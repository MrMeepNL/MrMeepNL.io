Especially if you are a console peasant, you may scuff at this subculture, without understanding its foundation. But it does have one, as there are many reasons why PCs are the superior physical platform to use.

  • Freedom of use
  • Modularity
  • Use cases

Freedom of use

One of the most logical advantages of PCs is that when you buy them, they are yours to use, tweak,and destroy. Even if you don't build your PC yourself and get it from an OEM as a pre-built, it is yours to keep. This freedom of use can be taken even further, if you use Linux instead of Windows. Sure, Windows works, but Linux gives you the ultimate freedom, at the cost of most times simplicity, which is why Linux is daunting at a first look. If you want to try it, check out r/linux or any linux distro subreddit. A PC, unlike a console, can be customized to your liking. If you want screaming RGB LEDS everywhere, you can. If you want to add an NVME SSD for faster boot times, you can. This is part of what makes the early 21st century the golden age for PCs, as we have reached the middle ground between no and full modularity.

Use cases

What can you use a console for? Gaming, mabye watching a movie. What can you use a modern smartphone for? A lot, but not high-processing-power demanding tasks, like video-editing. Sure, putting a filer on a video is also video-editing, we mean serious video-editing. A PC? It can do whatever you want it do. You can decide to spend a lot more on a CPU and RAM and be able to do serious progamming, or get a great GPU and play any modern title at more than just the 60FPS consoles and mobile phones struggle to reach, with great graphical features, like Adaptive Sync, Ray Tracing in every variant, and much more.

If you need to have 50TB of storage for your audiovisual products, you can just buy a NAS, and connect it to your PC. And if you have a tight budget, a $500 PC can do wonders, seriously. For gamers it is even more wonderful. You're no longer stuck with a bland store full of $60 exclusives, but with multiple websites like Steam and GOG, which include thousands of free-to-play games. And most of these aren't freemium either. And then look at modding communities. Do you know of any well-established PS4 game with mods? Probably not, and I don't know any either. PC games, on the other hand, commonly have mod support, and with at least unlocked directories, most games can even work on third-party software, created by the community. A great example is Terraria's modding community, which nowadays mostly runs with TModLoader, a reliable, actually good looking software, with an not even very daunting installation process. I'm not the first to state that modding can keep game communities alive, why else would we be playing G-Mod, Minecraft or GTA V after so many years?
